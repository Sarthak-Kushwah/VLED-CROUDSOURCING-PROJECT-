1. About the internship

The Vicharanashala internship is a structured, self-paced research internship programme run by the Vicharanashala Lab at IIT Ropar. It focuses on Applied AI and open-source software engineering. Interns work on real-world projects, learn through coursework, and contribute to open-source repositories — all remotely.

VINS stands for Vicharanashala INternship (online/Self-paced). It is the online, self-paced track of the internship where you complete coursework, daily standups, and project work remotely at your own pace within the programme timeline.

VINS has multiple phases. Phase 1 involves coursework and learning fundamentals through the Vibe LMS platform. Phase 2 involves team-based project work. Badges represent milestones and achievements — they indicate your progress through the programme and are visible on your dashboard.

The internship is primarily for current undergraduate and postgraduate students from any college in India. Alumni who have recently graduated (within the last year) may also be eligible depending on the cohort. Check the current cycle's eligibility criteria on the Overview page.

9. Rosetta — your internship journal

Rosetta is your daily internship journal — a structured reflection tool where you document your learning, challenges, and progress each day. It's a core part of the internship experience designed to build a "thinking routine."

No, it's not busywork. Rosetta is designed to help you develop metacognitive skills — the ability to think about your own thinking. Research shows that daily reflection significantly improves learning outcomes, problem-solving ability, and professional growth.

A thinking routine is a structured pattern of reflection that becomes habitual over time. In Rosetta, this means regularly asking yourself: What did I learn? What challenged me? What will I do differently? This builds self-awareness and continuous improvement habits.

Your Rosetta journal is accessible from your Samagama dashboard. It will be activated on your internship start date. You'll see daily prompts that guide your reflection entries.

Each day, open your Rosetta journal, read the prompt, and write a thoughtful reflection. It's best done at the end of your work session when your experiences are fresh. Aim to write consistently rather than in bulk.

There's no strict word count, but aim for meaningful reflection — typically 150–300 words per entry. Quality matters more than quantity. A thoughtful 150-word entry is better than a superficial 500-word one.

The one rule is: Write it yourself, in your own words, based on your own genuine experience and reflection. Authenticity is the foundation of Rosetta.

No. Using AI tools to write your Rosetta entries defeats the entire purpose of the journal. The value comes from YOUR reflection, not generated text. AI-written entries are detectable and will be flagged, potentially affecting your standing in the programme.

Missing an occasional day is understandable. Try to make up for it the next day by briefly reflecting on both days. However, consistently missing entries will affect your participation score. Set a daily reminder to build the habit.

Yes, your journal entries may be reviewed by the programme team and your mentor. This is to ensure genuine participation and to provide feedback. Your entries are kept confidential within the programme team.

Yes, prompts may evolve as the internship progresses to match the phase you're in. Early prompts focus on learning and adaptation, while later ones may focus on project progress and deeper technical reflection.

Rosetta entries are automatically saved as you write them. At the end of the internship, your complete journal is compiled automatically. There's no separate submission step — just ensure all your daily entries are completed.

11. Spurti Points

Spurti Points (SP) are a composite metric that measures your overall participation and engagement in the internship. They are calculated based on multiple factors including attendance, coursework progress, journal entries, and active participation.

SP is continuously evolving. The calculation methodology may be refined across cohorts based on feedback and programme needs. The current version reflects the best balance of participation metrics we've identified.

SP is an indicator, not the sole determinant of your internship outcome. Focus on genuine learning and consistent participation rather than gaming the number. If your SP is healthy, it means you're on track. If it's low, it's a signal to increase your engagement.

Consistently low SP may trigger a warning and review. If participation remains critically low despite warnings, the programme team may decide to excuse you from the internship. This is a last resort — the team will always reach out before taking such action.

A zero or negative SP indicates very low or no participation. This is a serious signal. Check your dashboard for missed activities and immediately resume regular participation. Contact the programme team if you believe there's an error in calculation.

High SP reflects strong engagement and may be considered positively for recommendations, project assignments, and recognition. However, the primary benefit is the learning and growth that comes from consistent participation.

Outcomes are determined by a holistic evaluation: project quality, learning demonstrated, teamwork, consistency, mentor feedback, and overall contribution. SP is one input among many — think of it as a health indicator rather than a final grade.

Strictly tracked requirements include: daily Rosetta entries, coursework deadlines, live session attendance (where mandatory), and project milestone submissions. These are non-negotiable components of the programme.

"Rolling basis" means your participation is evaluated over a moving window of time (e.g., the last 7 days) rather than cumulatively from day 1. This means recent consistent participation can improve your standing even if you had a slow start.

You'll receive a notification/warning on your dashboard. You'll have a grace period to bring your participation back up. If participation remains below the threshold after the warning period, further action may be taken including potential removal from the programme.

SP is calculated from multiple weighted factors: live session attendance, coursework completion rate, Rosetta journal consistency, standup participation, and project contributions. The exact weights may vary by phase and are detailed on your dashboard.

Yes, the programme team can make manual adjustments to SP in exceptional cases — for example, awarding bonus SP for outstanding contributions or deducting SP for code of conduct violations. Such adjustments are documented and communicated.
